Question - how to improve onboarding: 
Answer : 
This is one of the most important design decisions in HabitQuest.
Most health apps make a huge mistake:
They ask too many questions upfront.
Parents get fatigued and abandon onboarding.
Instead, think about onboarding in 3 layers:
Layer 1: 60-second setup (must complete)
Get only the information needed to immediately personalise.
Layer 2: Progressive profiling
Collect more information over the first 2-4 weeks through micro-questions.
Layer 3: Behavioural learning
The AI learns from what families actually do, which is more valuable than what they say.
The best AI personalisation comes from behaviour, not questionnaires.
The Goal
At the end of onboarding the AI should know:
Family Structure

The Rule I Would Give Developer
Every week:
Meals
70% familiar
20% adjacent
10% completely new
Example:
Family loves Indian food.
AI serves:
70% Indian
20% Middle Eastern
10% Mediterranean
This broadens food exposure naturally.
Activities
70% favourite activities
20% related activities
10% new activities
Example:
Football-loving child.
70% football
20% athletics
10% dance
This prevents boredom while maintaining engagement.
Most Important Data Point
After every meal:
“How did this go?”
:heart_eyes: Everyone loved it
:slightly_smiling_face: Mostly good
:neutral_face: Mixed response
:confused: Not great
:x: Never again
After every activity:
:heart_eyes: Loved it
:slightly_smiling_face: Liked it
:neutral_face: Okay
:confused: Not interested
:x: Hated it
This feedback becomes more valuable than onboarding and allows the AI to build a genuine “Family Preference Model” that improves every week. This is much more scalable than creating hundreds of rigid meal plans and gives the app the personalised feel that families will keep coming back to.

This is a very strong idea and actually helps solve one of the biggest challenges in behaviour change apps:
How do you teach families why a healthier choice is better without making it feel like a lecture?
The AI should never just give a meal or activity.
It should always give:
1. The suggestion
2. Why this version is healthier
3. One simple takeaway the family can reuse elsewhere

Critical AI Feature
After every meal:
:+1: Loved It
:-1: Not For Us
After every quest:
:+1: Fun
:-1: Boring
The AI should learn:
Meals
cuisines liked
cooking times preferred
ingredients disliked
healthy swaps accepted
family cooking habits
Quests
indoor vs outdoor
competitive vs cooperative
sporty vs playful
solo vs family activities
difficulty level
After 2–3 weeks the app should start saying:
“You usually enjoy quick Mediterranean meals and active family games after school, so we’ve chosen today’s plan based on those preferences.”
That moment is where the app starts feeling personalised and indispensable.
One More Feature I’d Add
Every meal and activity should have a:
“Why am I seeing this?”
button.
Example:
“You told us:
• You have 20 minutes to cook
• Your child likes Mexican food
• You wanted budget-friendly meals
• You enjoyed chicken fajitas last week
That’s why we’ve suggested this recipe.”
This creates transparency, builds trust in the AI, and teaches families how healthy decisions are being made rather than feeling like random recommendations. This could become one of HabitQuest’s strongest differentiators.
